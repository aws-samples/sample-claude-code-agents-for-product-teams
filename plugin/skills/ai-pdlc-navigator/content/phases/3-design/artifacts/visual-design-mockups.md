**Stakeholders:** _(none listed)_

## What this is

Pixel-level visual designs for the primary screens and states of the product — in the agreed design tool, aligned to the design system, and at the fidelity engineering needs to build. Mockups show what it will look like; the specification says how it should behave.

## Why it matters for the UI/UX Designer

You produce these so stakeholders can react to a concrete design instead of abstractions, and so Development has unambiguous visual targets. High-fidelity mockups also expose brand, accessibility, and content gaps that wireframes hide.

## Definition of Done

- [ ] Primary screens and their key variants are all present.
- [ ] Mockups apply design-system tokens (color, type, spacing) rather than one-off values.
- [ ] Imagery, iconography, and content are realistic (no lorem ipsum).
- [ ] Light/dark and responsive variants are included where applicable.
- [ ] Version stamp and change history are visible on the mockups.

## What it contains

- Primary screens and their key variants
- Applied design-system tokens (color, type, spacing)
- Imagery, iconography, and content in realistic form (no lorem ipsum)
- Light/dark and responsive variants where applicable
- Annotations that clarify intent vs. decoration
- Version stamps and change history

## Who consumes it

- Developer — builds to them; pairs them with the design spec
- Product Owner and Business Analyst — confirm the design honors intent
- Sales & Marketing — extracts preview assets for Define-era GTM drafting
- QA/Tester — uses for visual regression and launch-phase visual QA

## Common pitfalls

- Mockups that exceed the design system, creating one-off components
- Real content in English only, ignoring i18n space implications
- Handoff-grade mockups without handoff metadata (tokens, measurements)
- Mockup drift from the design specification so engineers don't know which is truth
