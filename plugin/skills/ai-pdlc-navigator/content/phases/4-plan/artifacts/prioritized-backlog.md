# prioritized backlog

_Produced by: Manage product backlog_

**Business outcome supported:** stand up the team, environments, pipeline, test strategy, SLOs, and work-ready backlog so engineering can begin productively on day one.

**Primary owner:** Product Owner

**Stakeholders:** _(none listed)_

## What this is

The ordered queue of everything the team might build, ranked by value and sequenced for delivery. It is the single source of truth for "what's next" — features, enablers, tech debt, and fixes all live here with explicit priority.

## Why it matters for the Product Owner

Your job is to maximize value per iteration, and that only happens if the team pulls from a backlog you actually believe in. An ordered, groomed backlog is the lever that keeps engineering on the highest-value work and the stakeholders aligned on the sequence — without it, you're arbitrating priorities in every standup.

## Definition of Done

- [ ] Items are ordered with value rationale captured for the top slice
- [ ] Acceptance criteria (or links to them) exist for near-term items
- [ ] Sizing/estimates from engineering are attached to ready items
- [ ] Dependencies and enablers are flagged inline
- [ ] Stakeholder commitments and dates are recorded where they exist
- [ ] Each item's refinement state (raw, groomed, ready, in progress) is visible

## What it contains

- Ranked list of items with clear value rationale for the top slice
- Acceptance criteria or links to them for near-term items
- Sizing/estimates from engineering for the ready items
- Dependencies and enablers flagged inline
- Stakeholder commitments and dates where they exist
- Clear refinement state per item (raw, groomed, ready, in progress)

## Inputs you rely on

- Approved requirements and MVP scope statement
- Effort estimates from developers
- Enabler backlog from the Architect
- Stakeholder input from workshops and the stakeholder map
- KPI definitions — what outcomes each item moves

## Who consumes it

- Developers — pull top items into iteration planning
- Project Manager — uses it to shape iteration plans and feature/epic status
- Business Analyst — maintains traceability from requirements to backlog items
- Stakeholders — see where their asks sit and what's been committed

## Common pitfalls

- Letting the backlog become a wish-list dump with no ordering discipline below the top slice
- Ranking by loudest stakeholder rather than value/effort/risk
- No "ready" definition — engineering hits items full of unknowns
- Hiding enablers and tech debt so the backlog looks feature-rich but isn't delivery-ready
